In 1977, NASA launched two spacecraft toward the edge of the solar system. Voyager 1 and Voyager 2 were programmed in a language called Forth — a language with no syntax rules, no classes, no frameworks, no runtime. Just a stack of numbers and a dictionary of words.

Voyager 1 is now 24 billion kilometers from Earth. It is still running Forth. Engineers at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory still send Forth commands across the void, and the spacecraft still executes them. The round-trip signal takes 45 hours. Forth has not crashed once.

What Forth Actually Is

Most programming languages try to hide the machine. Python, JavaScript, Java — they wrap the hardware in abstractions, in objects, in garbage collectors, in virtual machines. You write something that looks like English and the compiler figures out what to tell the processor.

Forth does the opposite. Forth is the machine, made legible.

There are eight things you need to understand Forth:

( everything is a word. numbers go on the stack. words operate on the stack. ) 3 4 + → push 3, push 4, add → 7 5 DUP * → push 5, duplicate, multiply → 25 10 3 SWAP / → push 10, push 3, swap, divide → 0.3 . → print top of stack .S → print entire stack ( define your own words with : and ; ) : SQUARE DUP * ; 5 SQUARE . → 25 ( that is the entire language. )

The Forth compiler is written in Forth. It bootstraps itself. The whole system fits in 2 kilobytes. That is why it runs on a 47-year-old spacecraft with 70-kilobyte RAM and no way to reboot.

Why NASA chose Forth: In spacecraft, silence is death. A language that crashes, leaks memory, or behaves unpredictably kills missions. Forth's entire execution model fits in one engineer's head. When something goes wrong 24 billion kilometers away, you need to reason about it completely. Forth is small enough to reason about completely.
