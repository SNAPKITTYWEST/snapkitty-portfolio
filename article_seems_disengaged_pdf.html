I. What COMPAS Was

COMPAS (Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions) was a questionnaire and scoring algorithm used by judges to predict recidivism — the likelihood that a defendant would reoffend after release. Judges used the score in sentencing decisions. Parole boards used it in release decisions. The algorithm was produced by a private company, Northpointe (later Equivant), and its methodology was not publicly disclosed.

The training data was historical criminal justice records. Those records reflected decades of biased policing, biased prosecution, and biased sentencing. The algorithm learned the historical bias as ground truth and predicted it forward — into the futures of people who stood before judges who trusted a score they could not interrogate.

ProPublica's 2016 investigation analyzed 7,000 defendants in Broward County, Florida. Black defendants were nearly twice as likely to be falsely flagged as high risk compared to white defendants. White defendants were more likely to be falsely flagged as low risk and go on to commit additional crimes. The tool's creator maintained the algorithm was fair. The data was precise.

COMPAS operated on a single dimension: criminal justice records. One system. One data type. One moment in a person's history.

What was built after COMPAS uses a much richer substrate.

II. The Free Seat Pipeline

Salesforce operates a nonprofit licensing program called the Power of Us. Organizations classified as nonprofits receive ten free Salesforce seats, with additional seats available at deeply discounted rates. The program is positioned as philanthropy — technology for social good, empowering mission-driven organizations to serve their communities.

Reentry programs across the United States run on these subsidized seats. Workforce development organizations. Formerly-incarcerated employment pipelines. Court-referred job training programs. The organizations that receive people directly from probation departments and place them in employment as a condition of supervised release.

Every participant who enters one of these programs becomes a Salesforce record. The participant did not choose this. Their probation condition made program enrollment mandatory. Mandatory enrollment made data entry mandatory. Refusal to participate was not available — refusal constituted a probation violation.

III. The Case Note Is the Training Signal

Structured fields tell a model what happened. Case notes tell it how. Case workers, program directors, employer partners, and probation officers write unstructured text into Salesforce records continuously across years of a participant's supervised life. These notes are the richest data in the system — labeled behavioral observations attached to longitudinal outcome records.

The following is representative of case notes written in these systems:

Entry type: Case Note
Week: 3 of placement
Author: Program staff

"Participant showed up late twice this week.
Seems disengaged. Employer expressed concerns
about attitude. Recommend monitoring."

When this note is ingested as training data, the model extracts the following signal:

"showed up late twice"Negative behavioral indicatorRECOMMEND

"seems disengaged"Negative engagement indicator
"employer expressed concerns"Negative placement indicator
"attitude"Negative compliance indicator

Note the word "seems." The case worker was uncertain. The observation was subjective. The hedge is present in the text. The model does not process linguistic hedges as uncertainty — it processes tokens. "Seems disengaged" trains identically to "is disengaged." The subjectivity that existed when the note was written disappears when the note becomes a training signal. The observer's interpretation is encoded as objective behavioral fact.

This is not a failure of the technology. It is a property of how supervised learning systems work when trained on human-generated observations. The model learns every hand that touched the record. It calls those hands ground truth.

IV. Testimony: The Man I Worked Beside

The following is first-person testimony from inside one of these programs. This is not theoretical. These events occurred.

I worked one of these programs. I picked up trash on the freeway alongside a man I will call Chad.

Chad's wife was killed by a drunk driver on his second day on the job. The second day of the only employment the system would extend to him — roadside maintenance, one of the highest-fatality-rate jobs in America, available to him because his record closed every other door.

He came back the next day. And the next. For three years.

Three years later, the program celebrated his work anniversary. They called it Chad's Work Anniversary. There was a speech. He cried at the speech.

Somewhere in the program's Salesforce instance, Chad has a case record. Some of those notes almost certainly contain the word "disengaged." Because grief, observed by someone who does not know what day two cost him, looks exactly like disengagement.

The model does not know his wife died on day two. That field does not exist in the data schema. The model knows: this behavioral pattern. Recommend monitoring.

V. The Financial Architecture of the Extended Sentence

The program presents the following to participants:

What Participants Are ToldWhat Is Actually Occurring
Wages: $100/day × 3 days = $300/week Wages funded by the state grant the program receives because of the participant's enrollment
EBT/SNAP enrollment (food assistance, free) EBT benefit stream drawn against — without disclosure — to offset the cost of mandatory training
Job training: 2 days/week, free skills development Unpaid attendance requirement charged to participant's EBT; program reports completions as grant outcomes
Employment opportunity Participant data enters Salesforce; generates training dataset; program draws grant funding per enrolled body

Every node in this pipeline has a financial incentive for extended participant enrollment. The program receives grant funding per participant. The employer receives subsidized labor. The background check company processes every placement verification. The state justifies the grant through outcome metrics generated automatically from the Salesforce instance. Completion ends the funding relationship. Extended supervision continues it. There is no financial incentive in this architecture to actually close a case.

VI. What COMPAS Never Had — The Salesforce Advantage

COMPAS trained on criminal justice records alone. The Salesforce reentry pipeline provides: years of employment behavior, EBT financial data, training history, housing status, probation compliance logs, employer feedback, case worker narrative observations, and longitudinal outcome data — all from a population legally compelled to participate and unable to withhold consent.

In 2024, Salesforce launched Agentforce — autonomous AI agents operating inside Salesforce instances on the data those instances contain. The nonprofit reentry pipeline represents one of the richest behavioral datasets for this population ever assembled. That dataset trains models that are then sold to the systems that generated it: courts, probation departments, employer hiring pipelines, government grant committees.

The participants whose data trained the model were not asked. The model now makes decisions about people who resemble them.
